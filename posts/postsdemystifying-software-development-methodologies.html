These processes are called **software development methodologies**, and they're essentially a set of rules and practices that guide a team from the initial idea to the final product. Let's take a look at some of the most common ones.

The Traditional Approach: Waterfall

Imagine building a house. You'd likely plan everything first: the foundation, the walls, the roof, and the plumbing. You wouldn't start putting up walls before the foundation is poured. This is the core idea behind the **Waterfall methodology**. It's a linear, sequential approach where each phase of development must be completed before the next one begins.

Phases: Requirements gathering, design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance.

Pros: It's very structured and easy to manage, especially for projects with well-defined requirements.

Cons: It's not flexible. If a client wants to change something halfway through, it can be a major headache and cause significant delays.

The Modern Way: Agile

In today's fast-paced world, flexibility is key. That's where **Agile** comes in. Agile is less of a rigid methodology and more of a philosophy. It values collaboration, customer feedback, and the ability to respond to change. Instead of building the entire product at once, Agile teams work in short cycles, called **sprints**, to deliver small, functional pieces of the project.

The core principles of Agile are:

  • Individuals and interactions over processes and tools.
  • Working software over comprehensive documentation.
  • Customer collaboration over contract negotiation.
  • Responding to change over following a plan.

A Popular Agile Framework: Scrum

While Agile is the big-picture philosophy, **Scrum** is one of the most popular ways to implement it. Scrum provides a specific set of roles, events, and artifacts to help a team follow the Agile principles.

Key Roles:

  • Product Owner: The voice of the customer, responsible for defining and prioritizing the work.
  • Scrum Master: The facilitator who helps the team follow Scrum practices and removes obstacles.
  • Development Team: The group of people who build the product.

Key Events:

  • Sprint: A short, time-boxed period (usually 1-4 weeks) during which the team works to complete a set amount of work.
  • Daily Stand-up: A brief, 15-minute meeting where the team syncs up on progress, plans for the day, and discusses any blockers.
  • Sprint Review: A meeting at the end of the sprint to demonstrate the working software to stakeholders.
  • Sprint Retrospective: A meeting where the team reflects on the past sprint and discusses what went well and what could be improved.

Scrum's iterative and incremental nature allows teams to quickly adapt to new requirements and deliver value to the customer continuously.
